### Day 4

#### Product Idea

A social media recipe-catcher app that uses the native OS share extension to seamlessly save and organize food posts from platforms like Instagram and TikTok, avoiding the clunky native "saved post" features of those networks.

|Chosen Model|
|-|
|Waterfall|

|Reasons|
|-|
|The core function—accepting a shared link, extracting the video/text, and categorizing it—is extremely specific and well-understood from day one, leaving little room for ambiguity.|
|Relying heavily on iOS/Android Share Extensions requires rigorous upfront architectural design to ensure you comply with OS-level memory and execution limits before any backend parsing code is written.|
|The definition of "done" is binary. Either the app successfully parses a TikTok/Instagram link into a standardized recipe format, or it doesn't. Fixed criteria make a linear testing phase highly effective.|
|You cannot effectively write the database schema for the recipes until you know exactly what metadata the social media parsers will extract, making a step-by-step approach logical.|
|Because this is a highly targeted utility tool designed to simplify tracking shared culinary experiences, locking in the requirements upfront prevents the project from unnecessarily ballooning into a massive social network of its own.|

#### Checklist

|Phase|Key Artifact|
|-|-|
|Design|Screens for the main app library and the small share-extension pop-up. Details the backend scraping/parsing logic and the database schema for storing recipes|
|Implementation|The actual frontend mobile code and backend parsing logic. Internal docs explaining how your app communicates with your database|
|Testing|Specific links from various platforms to test parser durability. A logged list of edge cases|
|Deployment|User-facing documentation for version 1.0. Provisioning profiles, screenshots, and metadata for the App Store/Google Play.|
|Maintenance|Dashboards monitoring parser failure rates. Logged user tickets when a social network updates its structure and breaks a link.|

#### Risk Flag

|Riskies Phase|
|-|
|Design / Implementation|

|The Risk|
|-|
|In the Waterfall model, you lock in your design early. The biggest risk is that Instagram or TikTok suddenly changes their DOM structure, API access, or rate limits right as you move into the Testing phase, completely breaking your rigidly designed parsing architecture|

|Mitigation Strategy|
|-|
|Design the system using a highly modular architecture (like the Strategy design pattern) for the parsers|


### Teach-back

Think of building software exactly like organizing a classic Sunday barbecue with friends. Nobody just goes to the butcher, grabs random meat, and wanders into the woods without a plan, right? We follow a specific order. That order is the SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle).

First comes the Requirements phase: The group discusses, 'How many people are coming? Are we having chicken or meatballs? What's our budget?' You figure out exactly what is needed.

Next is the Design phase: 'Which picnic area are we going to? Who is going to marinate the meat, and who is buying the charcoal?' You divide the tasks and plan the architecture.

Then the Implementation begins: The grill master steps up, lights the fire, and the meat starts sizzling. This is where the actual coding happens and the real sweat is shed.

Of course, you don't just blindly serve the food. There is a Testing phase: The grill master cuts a small piece of the first batch to taste it—'Is there enough salt? Is it cooked all the way through?' If something is missing, they adjust the seasoning and fix it.

If everything is perfect, Deployment begins: The cooked meat is brought to the table and served onto everyone's plates. The guests start eating, meaning your application is now live!

Finally, there is Maintenance: After the main meal, tea is brewed in the semaver (samovar), the dying embers are stoked, or missing napkins are replenished. Software is just like that; once it's live, it always needs a bit of 'tea service'—meaning minor bug fixes, updates, and continuous support."
